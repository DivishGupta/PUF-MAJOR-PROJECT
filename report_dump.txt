[42] ABSTRACT
[43] Physical Unclonable Functions (PUFs) have emerged as a promising hardware security primitive for device authentication, secure key generation, and cryptographic protocols by exploiting manufacturing process variations. Among delay-based PUFs, the Arbiter PUF provides a simple structure but is highly vulnerable to Machine Learning (ML) modeling attacks due to its linear delay behavior. To mitigate this threat, non-linear compositions such as the XOR Arbiter PUF are introduced. This project designs and implements a modular, config-driven simulation framework in Python to evaluate the cryptographic strength of single-chain and XOR-composed Arbiter PUFs against ML attacks. Using a full-stack platform (Vite/TypeScript frontend, Fast-API/Python backend, and SQLite database), we simulate challenges up to 256 stages, XOR orders up to 8, and varying environmental Gaussian noise. We perform systematic evaluations using Logistic Regression, Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Support Vector Machines (SVMs), and Random Forests under diverse Challenge-Response Pair (CRP) configurations. Our empirical results verify that while a single Arbiter PUF (k=1) is easily modeled with >90% accuracy, increasing the XOR order (k >= 3) and introducing noise exponentially degrades attack performance, dropping the modeling accuracy to the random guessing baseline (~50%) under standard constraints. The framework provides a sandbox to analyze the security-reliability trade-offs essential for designing secure PUF-based authentication protocols.

[175] CHAPTER 1 – INTRODUCTION
[176] 1.1 Background on Hardware Security
[177] In the modern era of ubiquitous computing, the Internet of Things (IoT), and resource-constrained edge devices, hardware security has transitioned from a specialized niche into a foundational pillar of the broader cybersecurity landscape. The proliferation of connected devices—spanning smart sensors, wearables, autonomous systems, and industrial controllers—has dramatically expanded the attack surface available to malicious adversaries. Traditional security models rely heavily on software-based cryptographic protocols that assume the absolute integrity of the underlying hardware platform. However, in contemporary supply chains and deployment environments, this assumption is increasingly difficult to guarantee.
[178] Conventionally, cryptographic algorithms depend on secret keys stored in non-volatile memories such as EEPROM or Flash memory. This static key storage model introduces critical physical vulnerabilities:
[179] Invasive Physical Attacks: Methods like micro-probing can physically expose internal data buses.
[180] Side-Channel Analysis (SCA): Non-invasive techniques such as Differential Power Analysis (DPA) and Electromagnetic Analysis (EMA) leak key bits through power consumption and radiation.
[181] Fault Injection & Debug Exploits: Clock/voltage glitching and JTAG boundary scan exploits can force the chip into revealing its internal states.
[182] Once the persistent key is retrieved by an adversary, all cryptographic protection collapses, regardless of the algorithmic strength of the cipher. Furthermore, in supply chain security scenarios, malicious hardware modifications (Hardware Trojans) can be inserted into integrated circuits (ICs) during third-party fabrication, enabling built-in backdoor access to stored secrets.
[183] These threats highlight a fundamental limitation: any secret stored persistently in a physical medium can eventually be extracted. Consequently, modern hardware security primitives must shift away from static, vulnerable key storage toward dynamic, tamper-evident physical root-of-trust (HRoT) mechanisms—systems that generate cryptographic keys on-demand without ever storing them permanently, thereby eliminating the persistent attack surface.
[184] 1.2 Physical Unclonable Functions (PUFs)
[185] Physical Unclonable Functions (PUFs) represent a revolutionary approach in hardware security by utilizing the uncontrollable microscopic manufacturing variations naturally present in every integrated circuit (IC). The idea of physical unclonability was first introduced by Ravikanth Pappu and colleagues in 2002 through optical scattering techniques, and was later adapted to silicon-based delay circuits by Blaise Gassend and researchers in 2005, laying the foundation for modern semiconductor PUF technology.
[186] During the fabrication of semiconductor devices using Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor (CMOS) technology, unavoidable atomic-level variations occur naturally in the manufacturing process. Factors such as variations in gate oxide thickness, random dopant distribution, line-edge roughness during photolithography, and polysilicon grain irregularities create small but measurable differences in transistor behaviour and signal propagation delays. Although all chips are manufactured using the same circuit design and fabrication process, no two chips possess exactly identical internal electrical characteristics.
[187] These manufacturing variations exhibit two important properties:
[188] Globally Consistent: All ICs fabricated using the same design follow identical architectural layouts and intended specifications.
[189] Locally Unique: Each chip develops unique microscopic electrical characteristics due to uncontrollable process variations, making every device physically distinguishable from others.
[190] Because of these characteristics, every integrated circuit can generate a unique hardware identity or “fingerprint” that is extremely difficult to duplicate or predict. Unlike conventional security systems that rely on stored secret keys in memory, PUFs derive their uniqueness directly from the physical structure of the hardware itself. This makes them highly resistant to cloning, tampering, and physical attacks. PUFs are widely used in modern hardware security applications such as device authentication, secure key generation, anti-counterfeiting, Internet of Things (IoT) security, and lightweight cryptographic systems. Their ability to provide intrinsic security without requiring permanent key storage makes them especially valuable for resource-constrained and security-critical electronic systems.

[202] Among various silicon-based PUF architectures, the Arbiter PUF is one of the most widely researched and commonly used delay-based designs. It operates by utilizing the naturally occurring manufacturing variations present in integrated circuits to generate unique responses for each chip. The architecture consists of multiple interconnected switching stages arranged symmetrically, where an input challenge controls the signal propagation paths through the circuit.
[203] When a challenge is applied, two electrical signals are launched simultaneously and travel through parallel paths across all switching stages. Depending on the challenge pattern, the paths may either remain parallel or interchange positions at different stages. Due to unavoidable microscopic variations introduced during semiconductor fabrication, the propagation delay of each path differs slightly from chip to chip.
[204] 1.3 Motivation and Research Gaps
[205] The Arbiter PUF is widely recognized for its simple and lightweight hardware architecture. It requires only a series of multiplexer stages along with a single arbiter circuit, making it highly area-efficient and power-efficient for resource-constrained systems such as Internet of Things (IoT) devices, embedded systems, and low-power security hardware. Due to its minimal hardware overhead and ease of implementation, the Arbiter PUF has become one of the most extensively studied delay-based PUF architectures. However, despite its hardware efficiency, the conventional Arbiter PUF suffers from a major security weakness. The response behavior of the PUF is strongly dependent on the cumulative delay differences generated across the signal propagation paths. Since these delay characteristics follow a predictable linear relationship, the overall behavior of the Arbiter PUF can be accurately modeled using machine learning techniques.
[206] An attacker can collect a sufficient number of Challenge-Response Pairs (CRPs) from the target device and use them to train machine learning models capable of predicting unseen responses with extremely high accuracy. Research studies have shown that even simple machine learning algorithms such as Logistic Regression can successfully learn the internal behavior of a standard Arbiter PUF using only a relatively small training dataset. Once the prediction model is trained, the attacker can emulate the behavior of the original hardware without physically cloning the device, effectively compromising its security.
[207] To improve resistance against such modeling attacks, researchers introduced the XOR Arbiter PUF architecture. In this approach, multiple independent Arbiter PUF chains are combined using XOR operations to generate the final response. The use of multiple interconnected PUF chains significantly increases the complexity and non-linearity of the response behavior, making it much harder for traditional linear machine learning models to accurately predict responses.
[208] Although XOR Arbiter PUFs provide stronger resistance against basic linear attacks, they are still vulnerable to advanced non-linear machine learning techniques. Modern learning algorithms such as Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Support Vector Machines (SVMs), and ensemble-based classifiers have demonstrated the capability to model and predict XOR Arbiter PUF responses when sufficient training data is available. As the complexity of the XOR structure increases, the difficulty of modeling also increases, but the exact practical security limits remain insufficiently explored.
[209] A significant research gap exists in understanding the precise boundaries at which machine learning attacks become ineffective. Factors such as challenge length, XOR complexity, training dataset size, and environmental noise all influence the learnability of the PUF, yet these relationships have not been systematically analyzed under a unified experimental framework. Therefore, there is a strong need for a controlled simulation environment capable of generating large-scale Challenge-Response Pair datasets under different operating conditions and evaluating the effectiveness of various machine learning attack models. Addressing this gap can provide deeper insights into the practical security limits of XOR Arbiter PUFs and contribute toward the development of more secure hardware authentication mechanisms.

[245] 1.5 Scope and Organization of the Report
[246] The scope of this project is primarily focused on the simulation-based security analysis of delay-based silicon Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) architectures, particularly Arbiter PUFs and XOR Arbiter PUFs. [247] This work does not involve physical integrated circuit fabrication or FPGA-based hardware implementation. Instead, the project relies on a carefully designed software simulation framework that closely emulates the behavior of real silicon devices. To improve realism, the framework incorporates controlled noise models that simulate practical environmental variations such as temperature fluctuations, supply voltage instability, and aging-related delay variations commonly observed in semiconductor devices.
[248] The project mainly concentrates on understanding the learnability and security limitations of XOR Arbiter PUFs through systematic experimentation and comparative machine learning analysis. The generated results aim to provide deeper insights into the practical robustness of PUF architectures in modern hardware security applications.

Strong PUFs, such as the Arbiter PUF, were initially proposed as lightweight, tamper-resistant authentication primitives that could provide exponentially large Challenge-Response Pair (CRP) spaces. However, researchers soon demonstrated that the mathematical relationship between the physical delays and the final response could be accurately modeled using machine learning techniques.

[298] 2.1.1 Machine Learning Attacks on PUFs
[299] Early groundbreaking work by Rührmair et al. demonstrated that standard Arbiter PUFs, as well as their variants (XOR Arbiter PUFs and Lightweight Secure PUFs), are vulnerable to modeling attacks. By collecting a subset of CRPs, adversaries can train ML models to predict the responses to unseen challenges with high accuracy.
[300] Logistic Regression (LR): LR has historically been the baseline for evaluating PUF vulnerability due to its efficiency in separating linear boundaries, which is characteristic of standard Arbiter PUFs.
[301] Artificial Neural Networks (ANN/MLP): Multi-Layer Perceptrons were later introduced to model more complex, non-linear PUF architectures (like XOR Arbiter PUFs). Deep learning techniques proved capable of breaking architectures that resisted simple linear regression.
[302] Advanced Classifiers (SVM and Random Forest): Recent literature has explored Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Random Forests (RF) to evaluate their efficacy in PUF modeling. SVMs with non-linear kernels are highly effective in finding complex decision boundaries, while ensemble methods like Random Forest provide robust models that are less prone to overfitting on noisy hardware data.
[303] 2.1.2 Existing Simulation Platforms
[304] While the theoretical models for PUF attacks are well-documented, the software ecosystem for simulating these environments remains fragmented. Libraries such as pypuf exist for generating synthetic CRPs and running basic evaluations. However, these tools are predominantly command-line interfaces (CLIs) or standalone Python scripts. They require significant programming overhead to configure experiments, lack persistent session management, and do not offer integrated graphical visualization.

[326] 2.2 Research Gaps
[327] Despite the extensive theoretical research on PUF vulnerabilities, there is a distinct lack of comprehensive, user-friendly software platforms tailored for hardware security researchers. The primary research gaps identified are:
[328] Lack of Integrated Visualization: Existing simulation scripts output raw numerical data. There is an absence of interactive, web-based dashboards that can dynamically visualize ML attack metrics (such as accuracy, training time, and decision boundaries) in real-time.
[329] Fragmented Experiment Tracking: Researchers often struggle to compare different ML models (e.g., comparing LR against SVM or RF) because existing tools do not persist experiment sessions or provide side-by-side comparative analytics.
[330] High Barrier to Entry: Setting up a local environment with specific machine learning libraries, hardware simulators, and evaluation scripts is cumbersome. There is a need for a streamlined, centralized application that abstracts the underlying complexity.
[331] 2.3 Problem Statement
[332] To design and develop a comprehensive, full-stack Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) Simulation and Analysis Platform. The system must seamlessly generate synthetic Arbiter PUF challenge-response pairs and provide a unified interface to evaluate their security against a suite of machine learning modeling attacks, specifically Logistic Regression (LR), Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), Support Vector Machines (SVM), and Random Forest (RF). The platform must also feature an intuitive graphical user interface, secure user authentication, persistent session tracking, and automated comparative report generation to streamline the research and evaluation process.
[333] 2.4 Objectives
[334] Backend Simulation & ML Engine: To develop a robust API (using FastAPI) capable of simulating Arbiter PUF behaviour, generating datasets, and training multiple machine learning models (LR, MLP, SVM, RF) on the generated data.
[335] Interactive Frontend Dashboard: To build a dynamic, responsive user interface (using React and Vite) that allows users to seamlessly configure PUF parameters (e.g., number of bits, dataset size) and select specific ML models for evaluation.
[336] Authentication & Session Management: To implement a secure user authentication system (SHA-256 password hashing).
[337] Data Visualization & Reporting: Automated client-side PDF report generation feature using jsPDF and html2canvas, which renders the session history and accuracy charts directly in the browser and exports them as a downloadable A4 PDF.
[338] 2.5 Methodology Overview
[339] Simulation & Data Generation Phase: Utilizing Python and numerical libraries (NumPy) to mathematically model the propagation delays of an Arbiter PUF. This module generates the X (challenges) and y (responses) datasets based on user-defined configurations.
[340] Machine Learning Pipeline: Integrating scikit-learn into the backend to ingest the generated CRPs. The pipeline is designed to instatiate, train, and test models (LR, MLP, SVM, RF) uniformly, standardizing the output metrics (Accuracy, Training Time).
[341] Backend API & Database Architecture: Developing a RESTful API using FastAPI to serve as the bridge between the ML engine and the user interface. An SQLite database is integrated using Python's built-in sqlite3 module with raw SQL queries for managing user credentials and experiment session logs.
[342] Frontend Development: Utilizing React to build a Single Page Application (SPA). State management is handled through React Hooks, and routing is protected based on username-based session tokens. The UI incorporates modern charting libraries (like Recharts) to provide interactive comparative visual analytics.

[399] 3.3 Module Design
[400] The system is logically partitioned into four highly cohesive modules:
[401] 3.3.1 PUF Simulation Module
[402] This module is responsible for synthesizing the behavior of an Arbiter PUF. It takes two primary parameters from the user: the Number of Bits (PUF size) and the Dataset Size (number of Challenge-Response Pairs). It mathematically models the internal delay characteristics using normal distributions. It generates binary challenge arrays and computes the corresponding 1-bit responses based on the cumulative delay difference between the top and bottom paths of the simulated arbiter circuit.
[403] 3.3.2 Machine Learning Attack Module
[404] Once the synthetic CRP dataset is generated, this module splits the data into training and testing sets. It instantiates the user-selected Machine Learning classifiers to predict the PUF responses. The module supports:
[405] Logistic Regression (LR): A linear model used to establish a baseline for attack success.
[406] Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP): A neural network configured to find non-linear patterns in the CRP data.
[407] Support Vector Machines (SVM): Utilizes hyperplane separation (with linear or non-linear kernels) to maximize the margin between '0' and '1' PUF responses.
[408] Random Forest (RF): An ensemble learning method that constructs multiple decision trees to yield robust predictions resistant to overfitting.
[409] 3.3.3 Authentication & Session Management Module
[410] Users register and log in using a secure authentication workflow. Passwords are hashed using SHA-256 before storage in the SQLite database. After authentication, the backend returns a username-based session token used by the frontend to maintain user sessions. Each simulation run is logged with associated experiment metadata such as model type, PUF configuration, accuracy, and training time.
[411] 3.3.4 Reporting & Visualization Module
[412] This module handles the extraction and presentation of data. On the frontend, a PDF generation module (using jsPDF and html2canvas) takes the session history and dynamically compiles a downloadable 'Experiment Report,' allowing researchers to export their findings for external review — no backend endpoint is required.

[421] CHAPTER 4 – IMPLEMENTATION
[422] 4.1 Simulation Engine Development & Dataset Generation
[423] The foundational element of this project is the accurate software simulation of an Arbiter Physical Unclonable Function (PUF). Because physical hardware fabrication is outside the scope of this software project, a robust mathematical delay model was implemented in Python using the NumPy library to synthesize the exact behavior of hardware variations found in silicon.
[424] An Arbiter PUF consists of multiple cascaded delay stages. At each stage, a challenge bit determines whether an electrical signal travels straight along its current path or crosses over to the parallel path. In a physical semiconductor, microscopic manufacturing variations cause the propagation delay of every single logic gate to differ slightly. To implement this in software, our simulation engine models the delay of each internal stage as a random variable drawn from a normal (Gaussian) distribution.
[425] When the user specifies a dataset size, the engine dynamically generates a matrix of random challenge vectors. For each challenge vector, the algorithm calculates the cumulative delay difference between the top and bottom paths based on the routing bits. Finally, an arbiter mechanism is simulated by evaluating this total delay difference: if the final difference is greater than zero, the response bit is evaluated as 1; otherwise, it is evaluated as 0.
[426] Fig 4.1: Logical flow of the Arbiter PUF delay accumulation algorithm, demonstrating how challenge bits dynamically route signals to determine the final binary response
[427] 4.2 Machine Learning Algorithms Implementation
[428] Once the synthetic dataset of Challenge-Response Pairs (CRPs) is generated, the backend feeds this data into the Machine Learning Engine. The project heavily leverages the scikit-learn Python library to conduct the modeling attacks. Prior to training, the dataset is automatically processed using the train_test_split utility, allocating 80% of the data for training the model and holding back 20% to validate its predictive accuracy on unseen challenges.
[429] 4.2.1 Support Vector Machines (SVM)
[430] The Support Vector Machine algorithm is implemented using the SVC class. Because the mathematical boundaries in complex PUFs (such as XOR PUFs) can be highly non-linear, a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel is utilized. The SVM maps the CRP inputs into a high-dimensional feature space, attempting to find the optimal hyperplane that separates the 0 and 1 responses while maximizing the margin between the two classes.
[431] 4.2.2 Random Forest (RF)
[432] The Random Forest classifier is implemented as an ensemble learning method. Rather than relying on a single decision path, the Random Forest Classifier is configured to build an aggregate of 100 distinct decision trees (n_estimators=100). This specific implementation was chosen because ensemble methods are inherently resistant to overfitting. Given that PUF delay data is essentially mathematical noise, single decision trees often memorize the noise rather than learning the underlying pattern; the Random Forest mitigates this by averaging the predictions, resulting in highly stable accuracy metrics.
[433] 4.2.3 Deep Learning and Baseline Models
[434] To provide a comprehensive comparative analysis, two additional models are implemented. A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP Classifier) is implemented with a hidden layer structure configured to perform deep learning attacks. The "ReLU" activation function is utilized within the hidden layers to prevent the vanishing gradient problem when training against complex topologies. Finally, a standard Logistic Regression (Logistic Regression) model is implemented to serve as a baseline, testing the linear separability of the generated datasets.

[457] 4.3 Backend API and Database Architecture
[458] The application logic and data routing are handled by a RESTful backend implemented using the FastAPI Python framework. FastAPI was explicitly selected over traditional synchronous frameworks like Flask because of its native support for asynchronous execution (async/await). Machine Learning training algorithms are highly CPU-bound operations; by handling incoming HTTP requests asynchronously, the API remains responsive and does not block concurrent users while a heavy simulation is running.
[459] To facilitate persistent experiment tracking, a secure user session system was developed. When a user registers or logs in, the FastAPI backend hashes their password using Python’s built-in hashlib (SHA-256) implementation returns a username-based session token to the frontend. Every subsequent request to run a simulation must pass this token in the Authorization header. Once a simulation completes, the backend utilizes Python’s built-in sqlite3 module with raw SQL queries to log the experiment details into a local SQLite database (app.db).

[502] CHAPTER 5 – RESULT AND DISCUSSION
[503] 5.1 Experimental Setup
[504] To rigorously evaluate the security vulnerabilities of the simulated Physical Unclonable Functions (PUFs) and ensure the reproducibility of our results, a standardized testing environment was established. All experiments, simulations, and machine learning attacks were executed locally using the developed full-stack platform.
[505] 5.1.1 Software and Hardware Environment
[506] The simulation engine and machine learning pipelines were hosted on a local Windows machine. The backend environment was configured using Python 3, leveraging highly optimized scientific computing libraries. The specific software stack utilized for the execution of the experiments included:
[507] API Engine: FastAPI (v0.110+) served via the Uvicorn ASGI server.
[508] Numerical Computation: NumPy (v1.24+) was utilized for generating the Gaussian delay distributions and matrix operations.
[509] Machine Learning Framework: Scikit-Learn (v1.4+) was employed to instantiate, train, and test the classification models (LR, MLP, SVM, RF).
[510] User Interface: A React-based Single Page Application (SPA) handled user input and visually rendered the experimental metrics dynamically.
[511] 5.1.2 Experimental Parameter Space
[512] To conduct a comprehensive cryptanalysis, the PUF variables were manipulated across a wide parameter space. The platform's frontend was used to configure the following variables for each session:
[513] PUF Architecture: XOR Arbiter PUF.
[514] Challenge Size (Number of Stages): Ranged between 64-bit and 128-bit configurations to observe the impact of exponential challenge space growth.
[515] XOR Levels: Configured between 1 (standard linear Arbiter) and 4 parallel streams to test the effectiveness of non-linear obfuscation.
[516] Dataset Size (CRPs): Scaled from 10,000 to 100,000 Challenge-Response Pairs.
[517] Data Splitting Strategy: A strict 80/20 train-test split was enforced across all sessions, utilizing a fixed random seed (seed=42) to guarantee that any variation in accuracy was due to the ML model's capability rather than data shuffling inconsistencies.
[518] Fig 5.1: The graphical configuration interface used to set experimental parameters.
[519] 5.2 Model Performance Analysis
[520] The simulation platform successfully executed modeling attacks using four distinct machine learning classifiers. The empirical data collected from these simulation sessions demonstrates clear differences in how each algorithm processes the non-linear noise and architectural complexities introduced by the XOR Arbiter PUF.
[521] 5.2.1 Logistic Regression (Baseline Linear Attack)
[522] Logistic Regression (LR) was utilized primarily as a baseline metric to test for linear separability within the challenge-response dataset. When tested against a standard, 1-XOR (single-layer) 64-bit Arbiter PUF, the LR model achieved exceptionally high prediction accuracies, frequently exceeding 95% with less than 10,000 CRPs. This empirically proves the long-standing theoretical consensus that basic Arbiter PUFs are mathematically linear and entirely insecure against basic regression modeling.
[523] Fig 5.2: Logistic Regression (LR) attack accuracy (59.65%) on a 64-bit, 2-XOR Arbiter PUF
[524] 5.2.2 Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)
[525] To bypass the non-linear defenses of the XOR PUF, deep learning was applied via the MLP classifier. The neural network's hidden layers allowed it to map the underlying feature representations of the XOR logic mathematically. During simulation sessions with 2-XOR and 3-XOR configurations, the MLP model demonstrated a steady climb in prediction accuracy as the dataset size was increased to 50,000 CRPs. It successfully broke the XOR PUF configurations that defeated the Logistic Regression model, achieving accuracies above 90%. The primary drawback observed during these sessions was computational overhead; the MLP required significantly higher training times compared to the baseline to converge on an accurate model.
[526] Fig 5.3: Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) attack accuracy (93.95%) on a 64-bit, 2-XOR Arbiter PUF
[527] 5.2.3 Support Vector Machines (SVM)
[528] The Support Vector Machine algorithm proved to be highly effective in cryptanalysis. By utilizing a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel, the SVM projected the incoming challenge vectors into a higher-dimensional feature space, successfully establishing complex decision boundaries around the XOR operations without needing the deep layered architecture of a neural network. The empirical results showed that the SVM achieved comparable, and occasionally superior, accuracy to the MLP model. Notably, the SVM often converged faster on medium-sized datasets.
[529] Fig 5.4: Support Vector Machine (SVM) attack accuracy (73.80%) on a 64-bit, 2-XOR Arbiter PUF
[530] 5.2.4 Random Forest (RF)
[531] The Random Forest classifier yielded exceptionally robust results. Because the simulated delay variables in the PUF rely heavily on Gaussian noise, there is a distinct risk of machine learning models overfitting to the specific noise profile of the training set rather than learning the generalized behavior of the circuit. The Random Forest mitigates this by building an ensemble of 100 distinct decision trees and averaging their outputs. While its peak accuracy on highly complex XOR PUFs (e.g., 4-XOR) was occasionally slightly lower than that of the deep neural network, its training time was remarkably efficient. Furthermore, its performance remained the most stable and variance-resistant across repeated simulation sessions with differing random seeds.
[532] Fig 5.5: Random Forest (RF) attack accuracy (62.80%) on a 64-bit, 2-XOR Arbiter PUF
[533] 5.3 Comparative Analysis
[534] To properly evaluate the severity of the machine learning vulnerabilities, it is necessary to compare the performance of all four classifiers against the exact same hardware configuration. The comparative baseline was established using a 64-bit, 2-XOR Arbiter PUF configuration, containing 10,000 Challenge-Response Pairs (CRPs) with an introduced environmental noise factor of 0.1.
[535] Fig 5.6: Comparative evaluation of machine learning modeling attacks against delay-based Arbiter PUFs. The graph illustrates the exponential decay in prediction accuracy for Logistic Regression (LR), Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Random Forest (RF) classifiers as architectural non-linearity (k-XOR complexity) increases, ultimately collapsing toward the 50% random guessing baseline at 4-XOR.
[536] The empirical data illustrated in Figure X.X definitively demonstrates the vulnerability of standard Arbiter PUFs and the protective efficacy of XOR-based obfuscation. At a baseline 1-XOR configuration, all evaluated machine learning models exhibit severe susceptibility, successfully predicting responses with accuracies exceeding 92%, which indicates high linear separability in the challenge-response space. However, as the architectural complexity increases to 2-XOR and 3-XOR, the linear mathematical modeling of Logistic Regression collapses sharply, while deep learning architectures (MLP) temporarily maintain higher predictive power by capturing hidden non-linear parity features. Ultimately, at the 4-XOR threshold, the strict non-linearity and exponential growth of the challenge-response combinations overwhelm the feature extraction capabilities of all tested classifiers. Consequently, the prediction accuracies for LR, MLP, SVM, and RF all strictly converge near the 50% random guessing baseline, proving that high-order XOR Arbiter PUFs provide robust cryptographic resistance against comprehensive profiling attacks.

[565] 5.4 Impact of Hardware Complexity
[566] A secondary series of experiments was conducted by manipulating the structural complexity of the PUF via the frontend configuration panel. The objective of this phase was to determine if scaling the hardware architecture could effectively mitigate the machine learning vulnerabilities observed in Section 5.3. Two primary variables were tested: the length of the challenge vector and the number of parallel XOR streams.
[567] 5.4.1 Scaling the Challenge Length (Stages)
[568] The first architectural adjustment involved increasing the number of delay stages from 64-bit to 128-bit. Mathematically, doubling the number of stages exponentially increases the total possible challenge space from 64 to 128.
[569] The simulation results showed that all machine learning models initially experienced a drop in prediction accuracy when evaluated on the 128-bit architecture using only 10,000 CRPs, as the larger feature space required more training data. However, when the dataset size was increased to 50,000 or 100,000 CRPs, the MLP and SVM models recovered their predictive performance. These findings indicate that increasing the bit-length of an Arbiter PUF alone does not provide fundamental cryptographic security.

[572] 5.4.2 Increasing Non-Linearity (XOR Levels)
[573] The most significant defense against machine learning cryptanalysis was observed when the xor_level parameter was increased. Every additional parallel arbiter stream that is XORed into the final response compounds the mathematical complexity of the system.
[574] When the simulation was scaled to a 4-XOR configuration, a severe degradation in attack success was recorded across all models. Linear models and ensemble methods (LR and RF) immediately dropped to near 50% accuracy. More importantly, even the advanced MLP and SVM algorithms struggled to converge. At higher XOR levels, the delay paths become heavily entangled, masking the internal physical characteristics from the final binary output.
[575] The data confirms that increasing the XOR complexity is a highly effective hardware defense. While an adversary could theoretically bypass a 4-XOR or 5-XOR PUF, doing so would require an astronomically large dataset of CRPs and massive deep learning computational overhead, making the attack practically infeasible under standard operational conditions. The primary trade-off, however, is that physical implementation of high-XOR PUFs requires significantly more silicon area and power, making them less suitable for highly constrained IoT edge devices.

[578] CHAPTER 6 – CONCLUSION AND FUTURE SCOPE
[579] 6.1 Conclusion
[580] The primary objective of this major project was to design, develop, and evaluate a comprehensive Physical Unclonable Function (PUF) Simulation and Machine Learning Analysis Platform. This goal was successfully achieved through the implementation of a modern, full-stack architecture utilizing a FastAPI backend for high-performance numerical computation and a React-based frontend for intuitive configuration and dynamic data visualization.
[581] Through rigorous empirical testing, the developed platform successfully simulated the delay characteristics of Arbiter PUFs and subjected them to four distinct machine learning classifiers: Logistic Regression (LR), Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forest (RF), and Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP). The key findings derived from the simulation data are as follows:
[582] Linear Insecurity: Standard single-chain Arbiter PUFs are mathematically linear and highly vulnerable to Logistic Regression attacks, often achieving prediction accuracies above 95%. However, introducing XOR-based non-linearity significantly reduces the effectiveness of linear classifiers.
[583] Efficacy of Deep Learning: Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP) proved highly capable of bypassing the non-linear obfuscation introduced by 2-XOR configurations, achieving attack success rates of up to 93.95% by extracting hidden features from the delay parity.
[584] Hardware Complexity as a Defense: The simulation definitively proved that increasing architectural complexity remains the ultimate defense mechanism. When the hardware was scaled to a 4-XOR configuration, even the deep neural networks (MLP) failed to converge, collapsing to an accuracy of ~51% (random guessing) despite utilizing a massive dataset of 30,000 Challenge-Response Pairs.
[585] Ultimately, this project bridges a critical gap in the hardware security ecosystem by providing an accessible, visual, and highly scalable software platform for evaluating PUF vulnerabilities, successfully eliminating the high barrier to entry traditionally associated with CLI-based cryptographic testing tools.
[586] 6.2 Limitations
[587] While the software platform successfully meets all primary objectives, certain limitations must be acknowledged:
[588] Mathematical Approximation: The delay simulation relies on generating variances from a mathematical normal (Gaussian) distribution. While this is an academic standard, it cannot perfectly replicate the complex, asymmetric environmental noise (such as temperature fluctuations and voltage droops) experienced by physical silicon hardware.
[589] Computational Bottlenecks: Because machine learning training (especially for MLPs and SVMs) is highly CPU-bound, scaling the dataset beyond 100,000 CRPs on a local machine results in significant execution times. The current backend architecture is limited by the physical hardware of the host machine.
[590] 6.3 Future Scope
[591] The modular nature of the current architecture provides several exciting avenues for future expansion: Hardware-in-the-Loop Integration: The most impactful future upgrade would be interfacing the FastAPI backend with physical Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) via serial communication. This would allow the platform to launch machine learning attacks against physical silicon PUFs in real-time rather than mathematical simulations.
[592] Advanced Attack Vectors: Future iterations could integrate Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) or Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) into the ML engine to test if newer AI architectures can break the highly secure 4-XOR configurations.
[593] Cloud Deployment & Distributed Computing: Transitioning the local SQLite database to a managed PostgreSQL cluster and deploying the FastAPI backend to a scalable cloud infrastructure (such as AWS or GCP) would allow multiple researchers to run massive cryptanalysis experiments concurrently without local hardware limitations.
